Magura HS33 Hydraulic Rim Brake

If you're into bikes, then you probably know about Magura Brakes.
Magura is a german company, which produces premium hydraulic brakes for bikes and motorbikes as well.
They are quite something else, and in terms of rim brakes I believe they are the best brakes in the world.
As a news flash: Magura recently introduced rim brakes for road bikes. They cost a lot and they look so cool, and probably stop you on a dime. They started to pop up even in the Tour de France. There isn't better marketing then that.

These “made in Germany” brakes haven't changed a lot during the years.
They got the great design right on the first time and they stuck with it.

In term of rim brakes (except the road bike version), they have the HS11, the HS33 and the HS33 Firm Tech.

  • The Magura HS11 are the entry level brakes, and they come on city bikes, trekking bikes, great stopping power.
  • The Magura HS33 are the most powerful, you can find them on XC mountain bikes, trial bikes, touring bikes loaded with a tone of kit, tandem bikes, unicycles. The difference between Magura HS33 and Magura HS11 is that the HS33 leaver has a bigger cylinder, thus being more powerful.
  • The Firm Tech model is designed to work only with Magura forks, they are the lightest because they have a different design, and they don't use mounting plates and brake boosters

How Magura brakes work?  I hear you ask.

There is a hydraulic circuit from the leavers to the brake pads.
Its simple: When pushing the brake leaver -the master cylinder in the leaver compresses fluid in the slave cylinders (which are mounted on the fork canti studs), and the slave cylinders pushes the brake pads.
The pads are pushed parallel to the rim and they give you the best rim braking performance in the world.

Magura HS33 hydraulic rim brakes versus V brakes

Off course there are some downfalls: the bigger price tag of the Magura's, the initial setup is a bit time consuming, and there is the grams factor. My actual Magura's weights 900 grams + 90 grams Magura brake boosters. A good V brake setup let's say: Avid SD7 (380gr)+ Avid leavers (350gr)+ some AL boosters (60) + wires and housing and noodles (50) it ads up to 840 grams.

A 160 gram penalty isn't that bad considering the benefits of the hydraulic rim brake:
  • better modulation
  • no maintenance
  • huge braking power
  • easy leaver action – one finger or two fingers
  • no more manual brake pad adjusting (the Magura has a TPA knob, wich adjusts the pads directly from the leaver )
  • better fiability – once installed you forget about them
  • the coolness factor – you have a great quality piece of german engineering, and its made in Germany not Malaysia
  • no more rusting brake cables getting stuck and gunked up wioth dirt and grease
  • no cables or housing getting loose

Almost all of the touring bikes made in Germany come equipped with Magura HS33 brakes. Magura has great support on their site, they have a forum, they have the manuals available at a click of a button. Also Magura parts are available, ensuring a long life for your braking system.

The Magura brake pads

Magura offers different types of pads, depending on the intended use and type of rim you are using:
  • normal black pads
  • racing red pads
  • normal black pads for rims that have a ceramic coating
  • racing red pads for rims that have a ceramic coating
You can also find a multitude of after market pads.

Some trial riders, instead of using the recommended MAGURA ROYAL BLOOD mineral oil, they use water, for a better modulation. How cool are these brakes :D.
In fact it is stated in the manual: “In the case of an emergency, cooking oil will work, but needs to be flushed out after the return to civilization.”

In the age of disk brakes, I still use my trusty Magura HS33's, and I don't think I'll be changing them soon.

Originally my german bike was equipped with Magura HS33. During the last 3 years I've tried Avid SD3 V brakes, Shimano Deore LX V brakes, Avid Single Digit 7 V brakes, and now I'm using Magura HS33's again – the latest design.
You can't beat the HS33, there's simply no competition.

This are my actual Magura HS33 hydraulic rim brakes fitted on my XC ride.
Initially they came with only the Magura mounting plate, but that wasn't enough to stop the flex in the fork. So I mounted some older model Magura Boosters for extra stiffness. That fixed the flexi forks problem, and made my front fork more stiffer. Light weight forks tend to flex a lot.

Here are side to side 2 different Magura HS33 models:

The Evolution and the EVO2.
The older model (Silver) has a different leaver, the TPA is also different.
The newer one (Black) has the TPA more tucked away, so preventing accidental brakage.
The Slave cylinders look the same. The mounting is different: the EVO2 has a mounting plate, the Evolution has some plastic studs/legs that stick against the forks preventing the movement of the whole assembly.
